# What Is a Model?

---

## Goal

The goal of this chapter is to understand the concept of a model in science and engineering.

By the end of this chapter, the reader should understand what a model is, why models are necessary, and how they differ from reality.

---

## Motivation

The real universe is far too complex to simulate in its entirety.

If we attempted to calculate every atom, every photon, and every interaction, even the world's most powerful computers would be insufficient.

Instead, scientists construct models.

Models preserve only the aspects of reality that are relevant to a particular question while intentionally ignoring unnecessary details.

---

## The Question

What is a model?

Why do scientists and engineers work with models instead of reality itself?

---

## Intuition

Imagine you want to study Earth's orbit around the Sun.

Do you need to know the color of the oceans, the shape of every mountain, or the number of trees on Earth?

No.

For this question, only Earth's mass, position, and velocity are important.

A simplified representation containing only those properties is called a model.

---

## Explanation

A model is a simplified representation of a real system created to answer a specific question or solve a particular problem.

Every model has three defining characteristics:

- It preserves part of reality.
- It ignores part of reality.
- It exists for a specific purpose.

No model is perfect.

A model is considered useful only if it is appropriate for the question being investigated.

---

## Scientific View

Virtually every branch of science relies on models.

Examples include:

- Point-mass models in classical mechanics.
- Spherical planets with concentrated mass in orbital mechanics.
- The ideal gas model in thermodynamics.

These are not reality itself.

They are simplified representations that enable understanding, prediction, and computation.

---

## Mathematics

Mathematics provides the language for expressing models.

Real-world properties become mathematical quantities:

- Mass → Scalar
- Position → Vector
- Time → Scalar
- Velocity → Vector

Relationships among these quantities are then expressed using mathematical equations.

---

## Cosmos Engine

Cosmos Engine is a collection of scientific models rather than a recreation of reality.

For example:

- `Body` models a physical object.
- `Universe` models the simulation environment.
- `NewtonianPhysicsModel` models Newtonian gravity.

Each class intentionally represents only the properties necessary for its purpose while ignoring unnecessary complexity.

The goal is not to recreate the universe perfectly but to construct useful computational models that explain and predict physical behavior.

The same architecture will later support experimentation with alternative physical models and hypothetical universes.

---

## Summary

A model is not reality.

A model is a tool for understanding reality.

Every model simplifies reality in order to answer specific questions.

Cosmos Engine itself is a collection of interacting scientific models.


A model is not judged by how much reality it contains, but by how well it answers the question it was created for.
