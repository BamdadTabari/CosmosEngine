# 11 — Foundations Review

---

# Title

Foundations Review

---

# Goal

To consolidate all the fundamental concepts learned so far and establish a solid bridge between the scientific foundations and the study of classical mechanics and Cosmos Engine.

---

# Motivation

Throughout the previous chapters, we explored a variety of concepts.

Some chapters discussed simulation.

Others introduced physical quantities.

Then we studied vectors, coordinate systems, space, and time.

At first glance, these topics may appear unrelated.

However, they are not.

They are all pieces of the same puzzle.

Each concept exists because it solves a specific problem, and together they provide the foundation required to describe and simulate the physical universe.

The purpose of this chapter is not to introduce new knowledge.

Its purpose is to connect everything we have learned so far.

---

# Let's Begin with a Simple Question

Suppose someone asks:

**"Where is the Earth right now?"**

At first, this seems like a simple question.

However, from the perspective of physics, answering it requires nearly every concept introduced in the previous chapters.

---

# Step One

Before describing the universe, we must understand what we are trying to build.

Cosmos Engine is not simply a game.

It is a scientific simulation engine.

That is why our journey began with the question:

**What is a simulation?**

We learned that a simulation is the process of reproducing the behavior of a real system through a mathematical or computational model.

---

# Step Two

A simulation requires a model.

A model is not reality itself.

Instead, it is a simplified representation of reality that captures only the properties necessary to answer specific questions.

Every scientific simulation, including Cosmos Engine, is built upon models.

---

# Step Three

A model without scientific laws has no predictive value.

Physics provides those laws.

Its purpose is to describe the behavior of nature using mathematics.

Cosmos Engine will eventually implement these physical laws inside software.

---

# Step Four

Once physical laws are defined, every measurable property must be expressed using consistent units.

This is why we studied the International System of Units (SI).

Without standardized units, scientific calculations become unreliable.

Meters, kilograms, and seconds form the common language of science.

---

# Step Five

With units established, we introduced physical quantities.

Mass.

Length.

Time.

Velocity.

Force.

These are all physical quantities.

Each has a precise definition, a measurement method, and a standard unit.

---

# Step Six

Not every quantity behaves the same way.

Some quantities have only magnitude.

Examples include:

- Mass
- Time
- Temperature

These are called scalars.

Other quantities require both magnitude and direction.

Examples include:

- Velocity
- Acceleration
- Force

These are vectors.

At this point, we discovered that numbers alone are not sufficient to describe the physical world.

---

# Step Seven

If vectors have direction, we must define that direction relative to something.

This is the role of coordinate systems.

The coordinate axes themselves are simply conventions.

Nevertheless, they provide the common reference frame required for every mathematical calculation in a simulation.

---

# Step Eight

Another question naturally appears.

Where do these coordinates exist?

The answer is space.

Space provides the framework in which every physical object exists.

Every object occupies a position within space.

---

# Step Nine

Knowing an object's position is still not enough.

Suppose we know the Earth's coordinates.

Have we fully described its motion?

Not at all.

We must also know **when** those coordinates were measured.

This is where time becomes essential.

Time allows us to describe change.

Without time, motion cannot exist.

---

# Everything Comes Together

Imagine that we want to represent a planet inside Cosmos Engine.

What information do we need?

First, we need a scientific model of the planet.

That model must follow the laws of physics.

Its mass must use SI units.

Its position must be represented by a vector.

That vector must belong to a coordinate system.

The coordinate system exists within three-dimensional space.

Finally, all of this information must correspond to a particular moment in time.

At this point, every chapter we have studied becomes connected.

---

# Cosmos Engine

Now we can imagine one of the most fundamental objects inside Cosmos Engine.

A celestial body.

Such an object will likely contain properties such as:

- Mass
- Position
- Velocity
- Radius
- Color
- Name

However, none of these properties are arbitrary.

Each one is directly supported by concepts introduced in the previous chapters.

Mass → Physical Quantities

Position → Vectors

Coordinates → Coordinate Systems

Space → Space

Time → Time

Nothing exists by accident.

Every field inside the software is rooted in scientific understanding.

---

# Why Haven't We Studied Mechanics Yet?

You might wonder why we have not yet discussed force, acceleration, or gravity.

The answer is simple.

Mechanics depends entirely on the concepts we have already learned.

Without understanding quantities, vectors, coordinate systems, space, and time, mechanics would become a collection of equations to memorize rather than concepts to understand.

A strong foundation makes the next stage significantly easier.

---

# The Road Ahead

Beginning with the next chapter, we enter the world of Classical Mechanics.

For the first time, we will answer questions such as:

- What is motion?
- What is the difference between speed and velocity?
- What is acceleration?
- How do forces change motion?
- Why do planets remain in orbit?
- How can these physical concepts be transformed into software?

This marks the transition from scientific foundations to physical modeling.

---

# Summary

Throughout this section, we established the scientific foundations of Cosmos Engine.

We learned that simulation begins with models.

Models are built upon the laws of physics.

Physics describes measurable quantities.

Quantities require standardized units.

Some quantities are scalars.

Others are vectors.

Vectors are defined within coordinate systems.

Coordinate systems exist in three-dimensional space.

Finally, every physical description becomes meaningful only when associated with time.

With these foundations complete, we are now ready to begin the study of Classical Mechanics.
