# Physical Quantities

---

## Goal

The goal of this chapter is to introduce the concept of physical quantities and explain their role in describing the universe.

By the end of this chapter, the reader should understand what a physical quantity is, what its components are, and why physics is built upon physical quantities.

---

## Motivation

Suppose someone asks:

"What is Earth's mass?"

Or:

"What is the speed of light?"

Or:

"What is the temperature of the Sun's surface?"

Each question asks about a measurable property of the universe.

Such measurable properties are called **physical quantities**.

---

## The Question

What is a physical quantity?

How does it differ from an ordinary number?

---

## Intuition

Imagine stepping onto a scale.

The display shows the number **70**.

Is that enough?

No.

70 what?

70 kilograms?

70 grams?

70 tons?

The number becomes meaningful only when combined with a unit.

Together they form a physical quantity.

---

## Explanation

A physical quantity is any measurable property of a physical system that can be expressed by a numerical value and a unit.

Every physical quantity consists of two parts:

- Numerical Value
- Unit

Examples include:

- 5 meters
- 10 kilograms
- 20 seconds
- 300 kelvin

Each is a physical quantity.

---

## Scientific View

Scientists describe the universe using physical quantities.

Examples of base quantities include:

- Mass
- Time
- Length
- Temperature

Examples of derived quantities include:

- Velocity
- Acceleration
- Force
- Energy
- Power
- Pressure

Nearly every equation in physics describes relationships among physical quantities.

---

## Mathematics

Mathematics often deals with abstract numbers.

Physics deals with quantities.

For example:

5 + 3 = 8

is mathematically valid.

In physics, units must also be compatible.

For example:

5 meters + 3 meters = 8 meters

However,

5 meters + 3 seconds

has no physical meaning.

Therefore, both the numerical value and the type of quantity matter.

---

## Cosmos Engine

Nearly every value stored in Cosmos Engine represents a physical quantity.

Examples include:

- Mass
- Position
- Velocity
- Acceleration
- Force
- Simulation Time

Every variable should clearly represent a specific physical quantity together with its corresponding SI unit.

Maintaining this discipline makes the engine more reliable, easier to understand, and less prone to errors.

---

## Summary

A physical quantity is any measurable property of the physical world.

Every quantity consists of a numerical value and a unit.

Physics describes nature through physical quantities, and Cosmos Engine follows the same principle when modeling reality.
